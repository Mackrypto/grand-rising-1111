## The Documented Record

Discussions about government biological programs and their impact on Black communities are often dismissed as conspiracy theory. But the historical record — established through declassified documents, congressional investigations, and court proceedings — reveals a pattern of state-sponsored biological experimentation that disproportionately targeted Black and other marginalized populations. These are not theories. They are documented facts.

## Project MKUltra (1953–1973)

The CIA's MKUltra program conducted experiments on unwitting subjects involving LSD, hypnosis, sensory deprivation, and other techniques aimed at mind control. Declassified in 1977 through Freedom of Information Act requests, MKUltra documents reveal that the program specifically targeted vulnerable populations including prisoners, mental health patients, and people with limited power to resist or report.

Black communities, already subject to over-policing and disproportionate incarceration, were particularly vulnerable to a program that drew its subjects from prisons and institutions.

## Operation Sea-Spray (1950)

In 1950, the U.S. Navy conducted a biological warfare experiment over San Francisco, spraying the bacteria *Serratia marcescens* from ships offshore. The goal was to study how biological agents would disperse in an urban environment. At least one person, Edward Nevin, died from a *Serratia marcescens* infection following the test. Hospital infection rates in the area spiked significantly.

This was one of at least 239 open-air biological warfare tests conducted by the U.S. military between 1949 and 1969, many in urban areas with significant Black populations.

## The St. Louis Aerosol Study (1953–1954)

Declassified in 2012, this Army program involved spraying zinc cadmium sulfide particles from rooftops and moving vehicles in low-income, predominantly Black neighborhoods in St. Louis. Residents were told the spraying was part of a smoke screen test. Sociologist Lisa Martino-Taylor's research has raised questions about whether radioactive materials were also included in the aerosol — a claim the Army denies.

What is not in dispute: the government selected poor Black neighborhoods for chemical exposure experiments without the knowledge or consent of residents.

## Lead Poisoning as Policy

While not a deliberate experiment, the government's decades-long failure to address lead contamination in Black communities represents a form of biotechnological negligence with devastating consequences. The Flint, Michigan water crisis (2014–present) exposed predominantly Black residents to dangerous levels of lead — a neurotoxin that causes permanent cognitive damage, especially in children.

Studies have shown that Black children in the United States are twice as likely as white children to have elevated blood lead levels. This disparity is not random — it is the product of housing segregation, environmental racism, and deliberate policy choices about which communities receive clean water, lead abatement, and environmental protection.

## Sickle Cell Screening and Discrimination

In the 1970s, mass sickle cell screening programs — ostensibly designed to help Black Americans — were instead used to discriminate. People identified as sickle cell carriers (who do not have the disease) were denied employment, insurance, and military service. The Air Force Academy barred carriers from admission. Several states passed mandatory screening laws without ensuring adequate genetic counseling or privacy protections.

A medical condition was transformed into a tool of exclusion — demonstrating how biotechnology in the hands of biased institutions can harm the very populations it claims to serve.

## The Pattern of Accountability

In each of these cases, the full truth emerged only through persistent activism, investigative journalism, FOIA requests, and congressional investigations — often decades after the harm was done. The Tuskegee Study ran for 40 years before being exposed. MKUltra documents were almost entirely destroyed before a partial cache was discovered. The St. Louis experiments remained classified for nearly 60 years.

This pattern raises an uncomfortable but necessary question: if these are the programs we know about, what remains classified? The question is not paranoia — it is the logical conclusion of a documented historical pattern.

## Moving Forward

Acknowledging this history is not about fostering distrust for its own sake. It is about understanding why Black communities' skepticism toward government health programs — including vaccine campaigns — has rational, historical foundations. Building trust requires accountability, transparency, and genuine community partnership — not dismissing legitimate concerns as ignorance or conspiracy thinking.
