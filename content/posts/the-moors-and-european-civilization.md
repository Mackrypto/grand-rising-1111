## The Inconvenient History

From 711 to 1492 CE, the Moors controlled significant portions of the Iberian Peninsula — modern-day Spain and Portugal — and their influence extended across Europe. This nearly eight-century presence transformed European civilization in ways that mainstream historical narratives consistently minimize or attribute to other sources.

## Who Were the Moors?

The term "Moor" was used by Europeans to describe the Muslim inhabitants of the Maghreb, the Iberian Peninsula, Sicily, and Malta during the Middle Ages. The Moors were a diverse group including Berbers, Arabs, and sub-Saharan Africans. Contemporary European sources frequently described them as Black. Shakespeare's Othello, "the Moor of Venice," is explicitly described as Black — reflecting the common European understanding of the term.

Historical artwork, manuscript illustrations, and written descriptions from the period consistently depict the Moors as dark-skinned people. The attempt to retroactively "lighten" the Moors is a modern phenomenon driven by the same impulse that whitewashed ancient Egypt.

## What the Moors Brought to Europe

When the Moors arrived in Iberia, much of Europe was in the depths of the Dark Ages. Literacy was rare, hygiene was virtually nonexistent, and scientific inquiry had largely stagnated. The Moors introduced:

### Mathematics and Science
- The Arabic numeral system (0-9), which replaced cumbersome Roman numerals
- Algebra (from the Arabic *al-jabr*)
- Advances in astronomy, including improved astrolabes
- Chemistry (from the Arabic *al-kimiya*)
- Optics and the scientific method, influenced by scholars like Ibn al-Haytham

### Medicine
- Surgical techniques documented by Abu al-Qasim al-Zahrawi (Albucasis), whose *Kitab al-Tasrif* was used as a medical textbook in Europe for 500 years
- Hospital systems with separate wards for different conditions
- Pharmacology and the use of anesthesia

### Architecture and Engineering
- The Great Mosque of Córdoba and the Alhambra palace — architectural masterpieces that still stand
- Advanced irrigation systems that transformed arid land into productive agriculture
- Urban planning including paved streets, street lighting, and public baths

### Hygiene and Public Health
- Public bathing culture at a time when many Europeans considered bathing unhealthy
- Sewage systems and clean water infrastructure
- Soap production and personal hygiene practices

## The Library at Córdoba

At its peak, the library of Córdoba contained an estimated 400,000 to 500,000 volumes — at a time when the largest library in Christian Europe held perhaps 400 books. The city had 70 public libraries, and literacy was common among both men and women of the Moorish population. This was the intellectual infrastructure that preserved and transmitted Greek philosophy, Indian mathematics, and Persian science to a Europe that had largely forgotten or never known these traditions.

## The Reconquista and Erasure

The Christian Reconquista that culminated in 1492 with the fall of Granada was followed by systematic destruction of Moorish cultural heritage. Books were burned, mosques were converted to churches, and the Muslim and Jewish populations were expelled or forced to convert. The intellectual achievements of Moorish civilization were gradually appropriated and repackaged as products of European genius.

The Renaissance — often presented as a uniquely European intellectual rebirth — was in significant part made possible by the knowledge, texts, and scientific methods preserved and advanced by Moorish scholars.

## Reclaiming the Narrative

The Moorish contribution to European civilization is not a matter of opinion or Afrocentric revisionism. It is documented in European archives, visible in standing architecture, and acknowledged by historians willing to follow the evidence. What remains is the work of centering this history in popular understanding — of ensuring that the story of European civilization honestly includes the Black and African peoples who helped build it.
