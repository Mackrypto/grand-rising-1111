## Beyond Skin Deep

Melanin is one of the most ancient and ubiquitous molecules in the natural world, found in organisms ranging from fungi and bacteria to birds and mammals. In humans, it determines skin, hair, and eye color — but reducing melanin to a cosmetic pigment fundamentally misrepresents its biological significance. Emerging research reveals melanin as a sophisticated biopolymer with properties that challenge conventional scientific understanding.

## The Biochemistry of Melanin

There are several forms of melanin in the human body:

- **Eumelanin** — the brown-black pigment most associated with darker skin tones
- **Pheomelanin** — the red-yellow pigment more common in lighter-skinned individuals
- **Neuromelanin** — found in the brain, particularly concentrated in the substantia nigra and locus coeruleus

Eumelanin is a remarkable molecule. It absorbs over 99.9% of UV radiation, converting potentially harmful energy into harmless heat through a process called ultrafast internal conversion. This photoprotective ability is why melanin-rich skin has significantly lower rates of skin cancer and photoaging.

But melanin's properties extend far beyond sun protection.

## Conductivity and Semiconductor Properties

Research published in journals including *ACS Nano* and *Advanced Materials* has demonstrated that melanin exhibits semiconductor properties. When hydrated, melanin conducts electricity through a proton-transport mechanism similar to certain biological ion channels. This has led to interest in melanin as a material for bioelectronics, biodegradable batteries, and biocompatible sensors.

The implications are striking: the molecule most concentrated in Black skin has intrinsic electrical properties that science is only beginning to understand and exploit technologically.

## Neuromelanin and the Brain

Neuromelanin, concentrated in the brainstem, plays roles that remain incompletely understood. It accumulates in dopamine-producing neurons of the substantia nigra — the region whose degeneration causes Parkinson's disease. Neuromelanin appears to bind heavy metals and toxins, potentially serving a neuroprotective function.

Some researchers have noted that neuromelanin concentration varies among individuals, though the clinical significance of this variation is still debated. What is clear is that neuromelanin is biologically active, not merely a metabolic byproduct.

## Historical Context: The Weaponization of Skin Color

The history of "racial science" is a history of constructing biological justifications for social hierarchy. From Samuel Morton's craniometry in the 1840s to the eugenics movement of the early 20th century, scientists produced reams of data that purported to demonstrate Black biological inferiority — data later shown to be fabricated, cherry-picked, or misinterpreted.

What these scientists systematically ignored was any research suggesting that melanin or other biological characteristics associated with Blackness might confer advantages or possess remarkable properties. The science was always in service of a conclusion that had been decided in advance.

## The Current Research Landscape

Today, melanin research is a growing field with applications in:

- **Materials science** — melanin-inspired coatings and adhesives
- **Medicine** — melanin nanoparticles for drug delivery and imaging
- **Energy** — melanin-based biodegradable electronics
- **Environmental science** — melanin's ability to bind heavy metals for water purification

The irony is difficult to miss: the same molecule used to justify the oppression of Black people is now being recognized as one of nature's most versatile and sophisticated materials.

## Questions Worth Asking

If melanin has semiconductor properties, neuroprotective functions, and remarkable energy-absorption capabilities, what else don't we know? And why has it taken mainstream science so long to investigate these properties seriously? The answers may lie less in the limitations of scientific method and more in the biases of the institutions that fund, conduct, and publish research.
