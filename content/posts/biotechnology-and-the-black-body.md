## The Foundation Built on Black Bodies

The history of biotechnology in America cannot be honestly told without confronting its deep entanglement with the exploitation of Black people. From the enslaved people subjected to experimental surgeries by J. Marion Sims — often called the "father of modern gynecology" — to the cells taken from Henrietta Lacks without her knowledge or consent, Black bodies have been both the raw material and the unacknowledged foundation of biomedical advancement.

## Henrietta Lacks and the HeLa Cell Line

In 1951, a sample of cervical cancer cells was taken from Henrietta Lacks, a 31-year-old Black woman, at Johns Hopkins Hospital. Those cells, dubbed "HeLa," became the first human cells to survive and reproduce indefinitely in a laboratory setting. HeLa cells have been used in research that led to:

- The polio vaccine
- Cancer research breakthroughs
- HIV/AIDS research
- Gene mapping
- In vitro fertilization techniques
- COVID-19 vaccine development

HeLa cells have been bought and sold by biotech companies for decades, generating billions of dollars in revenue. The Lacks family received no compensation and was not even informed of the cells' existence until more than twenty years after Henrietta's death. Her story, brought to wide public attention by Rebecca Skloot's 2010 book, is perhaps the most well-known example of biotechnological exploitation — but it is far from the only one.

## The Tuskegee Syphilis Study

From 1932 to 1972, the U.S. Public Health Service conducted the Tuskegee Study of Untreated Syphilis in the Negro Male. Six hundred Black men — 399 with syphilis and 201 without — were told they were receiving free treatment for "bad blood." In reality, they received no treatment at all, even after penicillin became the standard cure in 1947. The study continued for 25 more years, during which participants suffered, went blind, went insane, and died from a treatable disease — all so researchers could observe the "natural progression" of syphilis.

This was not a rogue operation. It was funded and supervised by the United States government.

## Genetic Research and the Black Genome

Modern genomics has revealed that African populations possess the greatest genetic diversity of any human group — a finding consistent with Africa as the origin point of all modern humans. This genetic diversity has made African and African-descended populations particularly valuable to geneticists studying human variation, disease susceptibility, and evolutionary history.

Yet the benefits of this research have flowed overwhelmingly to pharmaceutical companies and research institutions, not to Black communities. The 1000 Genomes Project, the Human Genome Project, and numerous genome-wide association studies have all drawn heavily on samples from people of African descent while failing to ensure equitable access to the medical advances those samples made possible.

## CRISPR and the Future

The advent of CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing technology raises urgent questions about who controls the future of human biology. If gene editing can eliminate sickle cell disease — a condition that disproportionately affects people of African descent — that represents genuine progress. The first FDA-approved CRISPR therapy, Casgevy, was approved in 2023 specifically for sickle cell disease.

But the same technology could theoretically be used for purposes far less benign. Who decides which traits are "diseases" to be edited out? Who has access to these therapies? At a list price exceeding $2 million per treatment, Casgevy is functionally inaccessible to most of the people it was designed to help.

## The Pattern

The pattern is consistent across centuries: Black biological material drives scientific discovery, the discoveries generate enormous wealth and prestige, and Black people are excluded from the benefits while bearing the costs. Acknowledging this pattern is not anti-science — it is a prerequisite for ethical science.

Understanding biotechnology's relationship with Black communities requires holding two truths simultaneously: that the science itself can be remarkable and life-saving, and that the systems governing who benefits from that science have been and continue to be shaped by racial inequality.
