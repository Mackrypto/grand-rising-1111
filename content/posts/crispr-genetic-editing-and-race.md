## The Promise and the Peril

CRISPR-Cas9, the revolutionary gene editing technology, has transformed biotechnology since its development as a genome editing tool in 2012. It allows scientists to precisely modify DNA sequences — cutting, deleting, or inserting genetic material with unprecedented accuracy. The potential applications range from curing genetic diseases to modifying crops to, theoretically, altering the human germline itself.

For Black communities, CRISPR represents both extraordinary promise and deeply concerning risks.

## Sickle Cell Disease: The Test Case

Sickle cell disease (SCD) affects approximately 100,000 Americans, the vast majority of whom are Black. It causes red blood cells to become rigid and sickle-shaped, leading to pain crises, organ damage, and reduced life expectancy. For decades, SCD received disproportionately less research funding than comparable diseases affecting primarily white populations — cystic fibrosis, for example, received significantly more NIH funding despite affecting fewer people.

In December 2023, the FDA approved Casgevy (exagamglogene autotemcel), the first CRISPR-based therapy, specifically for sickle cell disease. This was a genuine breakthrough — early results show the therapy can effectively eliminate sickle cell crises.

But the price — over $2 million per treatment — immediately raised questions about access. The people most likely to benefit from this therapy are disproportionately Black and disproportionately lower-income. Without aggressive intervention to ensure equitable access, CRISPR could become another example of medical advances that benefit those who need them least.

## The Equity Gap

The biotechnology industry is overwhelmingly white. As of recent data, Black scientists represent less than 5% of the biotech workforce. Black-led biotech firms receive a tiny fraction of venture capital funding. This means that decisions about which conditions to target, which populations to study, and how to price therapies are being made largely without Black representation.

This matters because priorities follow perspectives. The decades of underfunding for sickle cell research was not accidental — it reflected whose lives the research establishment valued.

## Enhancement and the New Eugenics

As CRISPR technology matures, the line between therapy and enhancement will blur. Today, we edit genes to cure disease. Tomorrow, we may edit genes to enhance intelligence, physical performance, or appearance. If this technology becomes available primarily to the wealthy — and if the wealthy are disproportionately white — we could see the emergence of genetically enhanced populations defined along existing racial and economic lines.

This is not science fiction. It is the logical trajectory of current trends unless deliberate policy interventions redirect it.

## Bioethics and Black Perspectives

The field of bioethics has historically centered white, Western perspectives. The ethical frameworks used to evaluate technologies like CRISPR often fail to account for:

- The specific history of medical exploitation of Black communities
- The ways "genetic normalcy" has been defined through a white lens
- The structural barriers that prevent equitable access to medical technology
- The cultural and spiritual dimensions of genetic identity

A truly inclusive bioethics would center the perspectives of communities most affected by both genetic disease and historical exploitation — communities that are currently the least represented in the conversations shaping this technology's future.

## What Must Be Done

1. **Ensure equitable access** to CRISPR therapies through public funding, insurance reform, and global health partnerships
2. **Increase Black representation** in biotechnology research, leadership, and policy-making
3. **Establish robust oversight** for genetic enhancement technologies before they become commercially available
4. **Center affected communities** in the ethical debates about which applications of gene editing should be pursued
5. **Learn from history** — the documented record of biotechnological exploitation of Black communities must inform current policy

The question is not whether CRISPR will reshape human biology. It will. The question is whether Black communities will have a voice in shaping how, and whether the benefits will be shared equitably or hoarded by those who already hold power.
